From Mockups to Production: Epic & Diver Implementation Plan

The dashboards on this site are requirements specifications, not the destination. They let us iterate on metrics, targets, and layout in hours instead of build cycles. This plan describes how each one moves into the systems of record — Epic for operational, in-workflow views and Diver (Dimensional Insight) for cross-domain scorecards and trending — and what has to be true before the numbers are real.

Guiding Principles
  • Mockups are the spec. Each dashboard here encodes agreed metric definitions, targets, and layouts (per the 7/10 referrals review and the 7/15 billing scorecard). Sign-off on a mockup = sign-off on requirements.
  • Build where the work happens. Worklists and same-day operational views belong in Epic, where staff already live. Monthly scorecards, trending, and cross-domain views belong in Diver.
  • Don't build twice. Specialty ops already maintains Diver dashboards (Behavioral Health, Nutrition). Inventory existing Epic Radar and Diver content first; extend rather than duplicate.
  • Fix data capture before reporting. Several mockup metrics currently live only in free-text notes or aren't captured at all. Structured capture changes are prerequisites, not afterthoughts.
  • Keep the sandbox. This site stays as the rapid-iteration layer for new requirements — mock here, validate definitions, then promote to Epic/Diver. Retire each mockup once its production equivalent reconciles.
Where Each Thing Belongs — Decision Framework

Epic-native

Data originates in Epic and users act on it during the workday: referral and imaging worklists, receipt-confirmation queues, denial/edit work queues, provider-level productivity. Tools: Radar dashboards, Reporting Workbench, SlicerDicer, Workqueues. Fastest path when the host instance already licenses similar Foundation content.

Diver (Dimensional Insight)

Cross-domain scorecards, month-over-month trending, and leadership views that blend Epic clinical/PB data with other sources: the billing scorecard, referral loop-closure by specialty/facility, internal access & capacity. Specialty ops already operates here — extend their portfolio.

Stay in mockup (for now)

Anything still changing weekly, plus future-state views tied to the referral-automation vendor decision — notably the imaging workflow process map (Phase 2 by agreement). Promote only when definitions stabilize and event-level data exists.

Epic-Native Quick Wins

Weeks 4–8
  • Referral and imaging worklists in Reporting Workbench: orders awaiting receipt confirmation (aging), referrals unscheduled >X days, appointments >24h without a returned result — the queues the process map identified.
  • Radar components for the daily operational metrics: outgoing referral TAT, receipt-confirmation cadence, denial/edit work-queue depth with aging.
  • PB metrics that are standard Clarity fare: denial rate by CARC/RARC grouping, clean-claim/first-pass rate, days in A/R, net collection rate, charge lag, write-offs by adjustment code (mapped to Dr. Knox's reason categories).

3

Diver Scorecards

Weeks 6–12
  • Professional billing scorecard (Dr. Knox's list): monthly, by clinic and provider — copay collection, outstanding-balance collection, no-show and after-hours fee collections, coding denials, missed charges, appeal & overturn rates, write-offs by reason, days in A/R, net collection rate.
  • Referral loop-closure scorecard: completion rate by specialty (high-value flagged) and by facility, days to close, failure reasons, administrative vs. clinical closures — trending monthly.
  • Internal access & capacity: time-to-schedule and time-to-first-appointment by service line (BH, Nutrition, Neurology, Psychiatry), specialist open-schedule/utilization, new-patient flow per provider — extending the existing specialty-ops Diver content.
4

Validation & Cutover

Rolling, per dashboard
  • Parallel-run each production build against its metric dictionary for one month; reconcile discrepancies (usually definition drift or capture gaps, not build errors).
  • Review with the operational owner (does the number match their lived reality?), then mark the corresponding mockup "superseded — see Epic/Diver" and stop maintaining it.
5

Rollout & Continuous Improvement

Ongoing
  • Training per audience: front-desk (collections scorecard), referral coordinators (worklists), practice managers (clinic scorecards), leadership (Diver).
  • Monthly PDSA cadence: plan the process change → implement → watch the metric → check for slip-back. The imaging workflow map becomes the monitoring layer for the automation-vendor rollout when that lands (its agreed Phase-2 role).

Cross-Cutting Risks & Dependencies
  • Host-instance access is the critical path. Build rights and Clarity access on an affiliate-hosted Epic take weeks-to-months; start the request in week 1 and design Phase 2 around what's granted.
  • Data capture precedes truth. Until outreach attempts, receipt confirmations, and fee capture are structured, those mockup metrics can't be validated — sequence capture changes first.
  • Vendor overlap. The referral-automation vendor will change the imaging/referral workflows this plan measures; keep Phase-2 builds metric-stable (definitions) even as the process underneath changes, and hold the process-map build until the vendor design is final.
  • Analyst capacity. This portfolio competes with the existing analytics request queue; without dedicated slots, Phase 2 slips. Use the analytics-requests triage to make the trade-offs explicit.
  • Duplication risk. Diver already covers parts of internal referrals; Epic Foundation likely covers parts of provider performance and PB. The Phase-1 inventory exists to prevent rebuilding either.
  • Compliance posture. Avance's conservative legal/consent stance means any AI-assisted or automated outreach components need early compliance review — keep reporting builds (this plan) decoupled from automation decisions so they aren't blocked together.
